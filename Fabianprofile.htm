FABIAN BADILLO

Fabian Badillo (he/him) is a senior majoring in Product Design.

Interest in STEM

"I was always an overachiever so during that time, my math teacher was like oh you’re really good at math, you should become an engineer. And that was when I started doing engineering or STEM camps for underrepresented minorities. That was where I gained an exposure, not within my community but outside of it. ”

“But my school is not known for its rigorous academics so our APs don’t really translate to doing well here. We don’t have engineering courses or our own STEM program. I know of schools near where I live in their richer suburbs where they do have engineering courses, a first robotics team, and research opps for high school students, but for me to get those opportunities, I had to look beyond my immediate school.”

Major

“I did some more research and a pre-orientation program here called the Stanford Summer Engineering Academy (SSEA), which takes 50 incoming freshmen and exposes them to different fields of engineering. We got presented to different fields of engineering. From that, I found Mechanical Engineering as the closest one I liked."

"I did an engineering course and internship over the summer over there but realized that Mechanical Engineering internships weren’t appealing for me and that more design-heavy ones were more fun. After working with designers, I realized I didn’t want to be a full-on mechanical engineer so I decided to switch to Product Design. "

Career

"I wanted to do more product management, being in charge of a team that is working on a project. I won’t be the one personally designing it but will be overseeing it so I do have enough of a design skill to see if it is a good or bad design. That’s kind of where my goals are. Right now, I will be working as a program manager for Microsoft so that is cool because I will be able to apply the things I learned through product design, but it’s nice that it’s not limiting me to just product design-related work.""

"I’ll be working in a tech company so I’ll still be involved in the tech scene. I don’t see myself leaving it. I even see myself going back to Chicago to work there, and even then they’re trying to set up their own Silicon Valley, doing incubators and startups and having tech companies."

Concerns about going into tech

"At first I was very concerned about not getting a job. A lot of my peers who come in with prior experience from high school, I definitely saw them getting good internships right off the bat, so that was a concern I had going into the space as a whole as a full time employee."

"After having acquired a job, I feel like everything that was holding me back in terms of getting a job was in my head when it just took the support of my community to get me to continue trying to get a job. In the tech scene, you definitely feel like somebody else will be better qualified than you are. "

Classroom Representation

"There are apparent realities that you have to accept, such as whenever I go into a class, it’s almost a norm that I’m the only Latino in the class. I’m interested to see the gender dynamics or gender stats for PD. It feels dominated by white middle-class women that are designers so it’s interesting because you have a group that is a minority as well so they are aware of what privileges they have. But it’s still very much the same story that I don’t see other people like me."

"But I don’t think I’ve ever been affected negatively by [the lack of Latinos in PD]. It’s more so something you get used to."

"I think SSEA gave me a false image of Stanford. I was like Oh, wow. There’s so many people of color here, people who have similar backgrounds as me, and we all are doing engineering. And I go to my freshman dorm and there are a handful of Latinos of here and a handful of other minorities. I go to an engineering class and it’s like if I sit in the back, it’s mostly a white-dominated space. It’s mostly white dominant and some Asian. That kind of was a little hard to get used to freshman year, now it’s whatever. Everybody’s just another ME or PD student, but that was the biggest misconception in that yes, there is a good chunk of underrepresented minorities that are doing STEM, but they are not always at the same place at the same time. If they are, it’s usually at a conference or club meeting."

Microaggressions

"Normally in the classroom or when we’re working on homework, it’s always related to math but there was one time where I was working in a group and things were a little bit weird. This was right before the presidential election when Donald Trump was being elected or when the voting was happening. We had a group dynamic in that we had one person who didn’t see any of the negatives of having President Trump elected, which was very different from another class where everyone was minorities or first-gen. They were directly affected by the comments against Mexicans coming into the U.S., all kinds of things. There were people in that class that were persecuted by the comments that are made by our president, whereas in this group, this guy was just saying,” Oh, it’s not gonna be that bad.” That’s something that was a little bit weird because I’m working on a group project and this guy thinks it’s okay for people like me to be sent back even though we were born here and raised here. I know is not a direct attack on me but it was something that kind of threw me off at first. I was like wait...you are willing to excuse XYZ and everything else that our president who got elected was doing. But at the time, it wasn’t necessarily too important for our project; the end goal was to finish our final project and get our grade so I was able to just get by and work with that person. But I don’t think he had anything negative towards me. I think he was just looking at the general sense of why he thought the president was fit to be elected."

Inclusivity for FLI Students

"The only thing is Product Design as a major isn’t necessarily the most inclusive in terms of socioeconomic status, not just Product Design but some Mechanical Engineering classes... For the Mechanical Engineering ones, you might have to buy your own materials and that’s where it doesn’t become the most accessible to students. For a class like ME203, where you build your own product, I guess you are a little bit discriminated against because you can’t afford to make big projects or go all out if you don’t have the money to fund it. For my project, it cost me $5 to make that’s because I chose the cheapest way of making a project. In thinking about money as a cost to the class, I only limited myself to a certain process for how to make things. Instead of learning everything I could do, I only learned one thing because that’s all I could afford... It’s really cool for people to build it but if you can’t afford to go through that prototyping phase, then it kind of limits you in a sense."

Support

"My friend group from SSEA, most of them are ME or did classes with me during my initial two years as ME. I’m still taking classes with them here and there, so having that support group got me through the academic classes...In terms of PD itself, the professors are really accessible. They post office hours when they’re there for, not just for class, so all you have to do is go, wait outside the door, and they’ll be able to talk to you. We have student advisors as well. That’s pretty cool to have because you can just email them and ask them a question, and it does not have that power dynamic like teacher and student."

Mentorship

"A lot of my mentors have come through my fraternity. My fraternity is centered around Latino culture so we have alumni who have graduated since 2004 to now. It’s good to have them because a lot of them have been mechanical engineers and have gone into program management so it has been pretty good just asking them about their career path and things they did to get to that point."

"Another neat thing is that a nickname that somebody has given me is Father of East Aurora. East Aurora is where I’m from, and I was the first one from my high school to get in. Since then, we’ve had at least one person get in for the next few years... Right now, you have five people from my high school which is from a low income area, doesn’t have the best AP classes, and is not known for its academics for sure. We’re still here and I’m going to be the first to graduate and I got a job at Microsoft. And for us, it means a lot because program manager is the highest position I could’ve gotten so it definitely means a lot to me to know that I’m making them proud."

Increasing Diversity in Tech

"I think the biggest thing is exposure. There’s a lot of kids who may not know what engineering is like or just know the stigma that engineering is hard. But if you can expose somebody to how fun it is or to something they might like, then from there, they’ll be motivated to learn more and put in the effort to succeed in the program."

"I think there’s a lot of things in place already to continue helping increase diversity. I think it’s just going to take time and more growth for sure, but I can see it happening."
