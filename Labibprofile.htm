LABIB TAZWAR RAHMAN

Labib Tazwar Rahman (he/him) is a sophomore from Bangladesh majoring in physics with a potential minor in CS.

Passion for STEM

"It mostly started from the get-go, just being able to open different devices in the home, trying to disassemble and then reassemble things was really fun. So breaking things down, learning their inner workings and then trying to understand what goes in the decision process of making any product or any tool was something that really fascinated me. So I would say childhood. My grandfather, he was a very hands-on guy, so he used to fix electronic devices at home. He used to do everything on his own so that also gave me a tendency to just try and fix things on my own.”

"I started taking classes related to STEM basically in high school, and I was particularly interested in math and physics because I really wanted to understand the world and how it worked, like I really wanted to know how stars are formed, how we emerged, and how all the laws of physics guide our life. All of these things I thought were best answered in a very structured manner in classrooms, so I started taking physics and math classes."


Demographics of Classes Back Home (Bangladesh)

“It was mostly fifty-fifty (gender-wise). I had been a little startled because in a few higher level math classes, the number of females were less than males. Bangladesh is a very racially homogenous country, so there were all Bangladeshis."

"I think there is a gender role that plays into what STEM subjects people opt into. There were always way more females in biology classes. I think there’s this tendency of seeing women as caregivers and that suits very nicely to the role of a doctor or a nurse, so more women I think were interested maybe by their environment or families or by themselves because of all these influences to pursue careers related to life sciences rather than physical sciences. In physics and chemistry classes I would say that there were almost equal levels of participation, but in very higher level classes there were more males, and in higher level biology classes there were more females."

Transitioning to Stanford

"It has been quite an interesting journey because it obviously came with its difficulties. There were moments where I didn’t really understand whether I should really pursue this because there’s a very small population at Stanford that actually studies physics contrary to what I thought. There is just peer pressure to study CS if someone’s studying STEM. I think that has really gotten to me. A lot of my friends, have mostly diverted to CS. It also feels like a lonely battle. For the departments themselves, I think there is a very stark difference in the way the physics or math departments treat their undergrads versus how the CS department treats their undergrads. I think the latter is more welcoming to all levels of backgrounds, whereas in physics and math people who don’t have enough background, or people who didn’t have olympiad experience or didn’t go to schools that could have higher advanced classes, I think are at a disadvantage at Stanford. It also seems very classist to me, because from the get-go there is this very big divide in people’s level of comprehension and also people’s level of understanding in physics and math, and that is very hard to just walk through. Most of my friends, and people who are from low income backgrounds are mostly in my classes, but people who are from better backgrounds than I am are in much more advanced classes because they had those classes in their high schools. That is certainly something that has been challenging. The disparity in the behavior of the faculty or the department towards us who are taking sort of the low level classes compared to those who are taking high level classes is also something that has been noticeable. So it has been an interesting journey, socially academically and administratively."

"Within the Stanford community I think it has been very evident that there have been a lot of initiatives by individuals or groups of individuals to make everyone feel more included and that has certainly been one of the contributing factors to why I’m still taking physics. If I had to fight against everything I wouldn’t- it would be more convenient for me to just study CS- it also is very interesting."

"I think there is a trend that they’re trying to be more inclusive, they’re trying to make sure that everyone has an equal footing, and also make sure that everyone feels that they can have a career and interest in physics. This is something I’ve seen in the CS department, where they don’t really care about what level you’re starting with, and they allocate a lot of resources to all levels, and that is also something that is happen in particular to physics because for the Physics 40 Series-- there are so many resources. There are office hours everyday for a lot of hours and the TAs are absolutely brilliant, so I think there have certain structural reforms that the physics department or the math departments are taking in order to address the problems. More needs to be done, but I’m very appreciative that they are taking newer steps to make people feel welcome."

Support Groups on Campus

"I don’t know about math all that much, but for physics, there is one group called PUWMAS (Physics Undergraduate Women and Gender Minorities at Stanford). They are very active. There is also one physics student-group SUPS (Stanford University Physics Society). The students themselves are very very warm, they are very lovely. The students have also been a very big contributor to my adapting to this challenge. I’ve been supported a lot by the students-- individually, and also as groups."

Memorable Experiences in Physics

I really like going to physics office hours. One thing that I have made sure that is constant is that from Physics 41 (Mechanics), I took Physics 43 (Electricity and Magnetism) and now I’m taking Physics 45 (Light, Hear and Waves)-- across these three quarters, is that I have not missed a single Friday office hours. It’s a very nice environment. Everyone is doing physics, and there are a lot of TAs to help. That is something that I really cherish, and I certainly look forward to going there every Friday, just solving physics problems with very helpful and lovely people."

Diversity in Physics at Stanford

"In terms of gender and race, the classes that I have taken have been very diverse, but I’ve heard that as classes get more advanced, it’s mostly male dominated. I can’t verify that because I haven’t taken those classes, but I have heard anecdotes from people who have."

"I do think about [the diversity in classes] a lot, and I do appreciate how diverse the classroom setting is [in the classes I’ve taken]. There are people of different gender identities, or different sexual orientations and of different races. It’s a really nice environment to be in, it’s very diverse and I really want physics to be as diverse. I don’t want it to be a white male subject, I want it to be very inclusive of all sorts of identities."

"I certainly applaud the diversity initiative being taken at Stanford, I’m pretty sure there is always more to be done, so there are certain student groups, there are faculty initiated projects that have made sure that people of different identities which are not very dominant in physics feel more included. I think it would be good if those are kept going but more momentum is added, and there are more programs under those projects’ banner, and there are more people, more resources and more events allocated under that. There is not limit to how good it can be done, but I think the footing has already been established, it’s just to continue progressing from there."

Exploring the Physics Major at Stanford

"Through the PUWMAS group I was talking about, I’m going to a conference in Houston, so they have allocated funds for me to do so. That has been very nice. The physics department itself sometimes does major nights and they have faculty dinner. The department has a lot of stringent rules and maybe there is a lot of bureaucracy that goes on, but the faculty members and the professors themselves are very humble and very caring. If I reach out to them, they are always very helpful, they’re always there to answer questions I have an resolve any confusion. They have also been very supportive of research. I did research this summer at SLAC (National Accelerator Laboratory). They gave me funding to do that, and they also gave me a lot of support, both in the research and also while getting to that. There have been quite a few factors outside of classes that have made my physics journey nice."

"I particularly chose to do this research because my mentor was my TA for two of my physics classes-- an astronomy class and also mechanics. Anytime I asked him a question, he never answered it straight on, but he would just would keep on asking me small questions that could lead me to the right answer. Although it took a lot of time and effort, I really enjoyed that entire process of getting the answer on my own, so I thought getting to learn or do research would be a very interesting and enlightening experience. It certainly has lived up to that expectation of mine."

"Apart from my mentor, my professor, the entire lab, they have been very very warm, very welcoming, very fun-loving. We had nitrogen ice-cream sometimes. We celebrated everyone’s birthday in the lab. We always ate together at picnic tables. The environment in the lab was very warm and welcoming to someone like me."

Continued Drive to Pursue Physics

"I really like physics a lot, and I have liked physics for a big fraction of my life. I really want to know more physics. I’m not satisfied yet. It is that drive that motivated me to be in physics, and I think there are certainly a lot of new areas to explore, so I’m still going strong in learning."

Career Aspirations After Stanford, Perceptions of Silicon Valley

"The dominant one is to continue being at school, so just to go to grad school, and to see where I go from there."

"I would certainly want to have a taste of how it [Silicon Valley] is, I’m not sure if that is something that I’d want to do permanently, but it would be very interesting taking a summer off from physics or maybe a year working in industry just to get a flavor of how that is. I know Silicon Valley has heavy immigrant influence. People I know who work in Silicon Valley have mostly been international students. From their perspectives I have heard that it’s quite inclusive. I am pretty aware of the various sorts of hidden and also sometimes explicit discrimination done against different races, nationalities, genders and sexual orientations, but I think that it is certainly better in terms of inclusiveness than a lot of other non-tech industries that probably have not had as much diversity as Silicon Valley."
