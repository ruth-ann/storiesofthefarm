MARTIN ALCARAZ JR.

Martin Alcaraz Jr. (he/him) is a third year Bioengineering and premed undergrad at Stanford University.

Major

"I decided to declare my major because I was always attracted to biology and the human genome, and I accredit this interest to my mamacita bella! Ever since I was a little kid, she forced me to watch documentaries, and this included things like the discovery of penicillin, the cell, etc., and I absolutely hated it them. I remember my mom forcing me to watch this and her rational being that I need to be educated so that I do not work in a tortilla factory or McDonalds or Bakery like her, and I remember crying about it. I remember she made me watch Discovery Health, like Untold Stories of the E.R. and Dr. G: Medical Examiner, and soon I fell in love with it and I am forever thankful that she made me watch it.”

Career

“I aspire to be a neurosurgeon and a neuroengineer – I guess that is also one of the reasons that I picked to major in Bioengineering. With regards to becoming a neuroengineer, I am very interested in spinal cord injury recovery on the cellular and molecular level – that’s a part of one of the labs that I am a part of at Stanford. I am also interested in working with brain tumors and brain surgeries for the fact that I had a middle school teacher who had a tumor and her entire right side became paralyzed and everyone bullied her; I know, that’s awful. Students would pick on her and tease her – I went to a bad middle school – but I empathized with her. I got to know her story and I became so interested in brain tumors and the whole process of removing them and treating patients with brain injuries. Furthermore, I just love the brain and it hurts me when I see people who are affected and cannot control the outcomes when they have brain injury – and it’s pretty common in areas that I come from because of gang fights or just everyday injuries, and I want to make ‘neurosurgeries’ readily available to them. I know that sounds naïve, but I am determined to do this in the future."

Discrimination in STEM

"I know that when I sit in a STEM or Bioengineering classroom at Stanford, I see a lot of white and white-passing individuals, that is to say Mexicans or Latinx people who are white. I rarely see Brown or Black people in a classroom, and furthermore I do see that these spaces are dominated by cis-males. With regards to myself, I often motivate myself by reminding myself of the people who came before me and how far I have come. Specifically, with regards to going to STEM one of my role models, who happens to be the top neurosurgeon in the country, is Dr. Alfredo Quinones-Hinojosa. His story motivates me and pushes me to continue to move forward. You should really look into it! It is absolutely amazing and inspiring as he is an immigrant from Mexico who went from community college to Cal to Harvard Med!"

Diversity in the Classroom

" Once I went to a talk at the Stanford School of Medicine and I asked how it was like to combine a PhD with a potential path in Neurosurgery, and I was told not to do it by the individual giving the talk and I listed a few names, of white people who had done it, and the guy laughed and asked for my background and said, “You can’t do it! They can, but not you. You won’t be able to; chose one or the other” and I was hurt; but I was also motivated. I don’t know if he was referring to my race or economic status, but … that motivated me even more to pursue a PhD/MD. But I guess the thing that shocked me the most was that it came from a faculty at the medical school."
