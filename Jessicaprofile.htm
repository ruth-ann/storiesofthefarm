JESSICA QUIROZ

Jessica Quiroz (she/her) is a Freshman fro anticipating a major in Mechanical Engineering. She is a Freshman Intern for SOLE, with a role in SLAE.

Major

"What drew me to the major, so my high school took a few kids to the SpaceX headquarters on a field trip, and it was really cool. We went inside and once I saw the rockets I thought: “I have to work on something like this. I just fell in love.”

“Everyone seemed like they enjoyed what they were doing and were passionate about it too [at SpaceX]. So, that really drew me to it.”

Career aspirations

"SpaceX. I really want to work there. Or Tesla.”

Approaching Inequality

“Being at Stanford is already kind of intimidating in a racial perspective, coming from me being a minority and being FLI here. So, I thought I would gain experience here on how to deal with it.”

“I know there will be people in the interview process who have bias, but I am not sure how to deal with that, since I cannot really change that.”

Experiences

“On campus, with certain groups and organizations that are not explicitly racist, but you can tell that they are bias somewhere or prejudice.”

“Where I lived was pretty diverse: there were hispanic and black people. The neighboring city was white. We were the poor city and they were the rich city - so I guess that is called racism because of the racial hierarchy.”

“[Racism on campus] is not explicit, but the looks some people give you or the way they act towards you are more rude.”

STEM at Stanford

“There is not a lack of [diversity in my STEM classes], not to be mean, but it’s one of the lower math classes, it’s Math 20, it’s not like Math 51 or CME 100. I think that is because minorities tend to be in lower math classes because we usually come from like low income places with low funding. But, there is definitely inequality in the proportions of people."

“Yeah, I will definitely be intimidated by [the lack of diversity in my future classes]. I know that there are people that are way smarter than me, but having them be male and white is also really intimidating because they have privilege outside of the classroom and it is kind of hard to deal with that. But, I have friends in my classes and later I am not sure if they will be in it, so it’s hard to tell right now.”

“I am not sure if [others are aware of their implicit racial and gendered comments]. It is implicit for a reason because I don’t think they are aware of it, but they should know that what they say has some impact or power."

“No [I haven't felt discouraged from pursuing STEM]. Maybe it’s because I am in SOLE, Society of LatinX Engineers. And in that im in this thing called SLAE - Society of LatinA Engineers. It’s kind of like a group where you support each other being in STEM.”

SOLE

“For Sole I am a frosh intern. I just applied, they interviewed me, and I got in. But, I applied specifically to be a Frosh intern for SLAE. And with that I have been doing work. We are going to collaborate with SWE - society of women engineers.”

“For SLE, we are thinking of bringing in high schoolers or going to high schools. SPecifically, working with minorities and females because in SOLE there are a lot of engineers who are minorities, but they are males. So the females kind of get intimidated by that. So I think that if we could get more girls in SLAE it will be better And with SWE, I haven't been to many meetings, but I Heard that they are not high on diversity. At least, that is what I have heard, So, I think we’re working to increase diversity in SWE.”

Personal Passion for STEM

“It started when I visited SpaceX. After that, I thought that I should probably do something in Engineering. After that, I kind of followed that path.”

“I think that was the only problem at SPaceX, most people were male. If they were female, they were usually white. The people who were colored, they were like in the dining area or janitors or something.”

Future

“I know since I got into Stanford and applied as Mechanical Engineering, a lot of the High School Seniors I am friends with, or Juniors, that they are kind of like inspired. It gives them some hope that someone from our community can do it too.”

Advice

“[Discrimination based on gender and race] is there, it is undeniable. Like, I’m not going to tell you its not there. But, I mean there are people that are there for you and are going to support you. There are resources here. It’s not impossible. It is going to be difficult, but it's not impossible.”
