JAY LIU

Jay Liu (they/them) is a junior double majoring in Computer Science and Chemistry.

Major

"I first started taking CS and chemistry classes frosh year. It was right away, straight up about a whole quarter I enrolled in Chemistry 31A and CS106B, and ever since then I’ve just been taking one CS and one Chemistry class basically every quarter. Both subjects just grew on me at the same time. And so Chem and CS appealed to me in two different ways, but at their core they’re both majors that allow me to do a lot with my mind and create and be creative, which is really important to me, but also have the opportunity to get really, really deep in theory and science and all that is very interesting and exciting."

Career aspirations

“I came into Stanford solid pre-med, and it’s been bouncing back and forth lately about whether I am going to stay on that path. Right now, at least at this moment, I feel more drawn to going to graduate school and getting really deep into a chemistry field in which I could apply CS practices, techniques, knowledge."

Race/gender discrimination

"Gender is something that still impacts me a lot that I see very often. I identify as genderqueer, non-binary. I use they/them pronouns and so it always stands out to me like a lot when a form is like what gender are you? Male or female? And it stands out to me when specifically the questionnaire asks you the gender question and it’s not clear why they would need that information and how they would use that information. Especially when they don’t give you leeway to provide an alternative answer, it’s very obvious for me."

"In terms of gender microaggressions, it’s not only transphobia but also just sexism at its most basic level. Often unfortunately, in my encounters in the CS department, especially during office hours, where I feel like pressure is high, people are stressed, and sometimes attitudes or demeanors get out that wouldn’t normally get out in a more controlled setting. But just in a space where everyone is struggling to complete their assignment or understand the concept, it’s very clear when someone is getting testy or acting arrogant or condescending towards you in particular, maybe because of your gender, your race, or anything external perceived by you. It’s very clear when they’re doing that to you and not to the next person they’re talking to."

"But there’s also been good things that I’ve noticed, especially within the CS department. Certain classes are very conscious about being gender inclusive. Hiring a diverse group of TA’s for instance can make all the difference or making room for gender-non-specified designations on forms or even as simple as asking for preferred name versus a given name on a test so that it’s clear how a non-binary person or a trans person who has changed their name wants to identify. Just like making things slightly easier for people in ways that you might not necessarily think makes a difference but in reality it’s like night and day for a queer person."

Passion for STEM

"The thing that kept the STEM door open for me, the thing that didn’t prevent it being shut all the way, I’m very lucky to have had in my life is the influence of probably my dad’s strongest. He is a computer scientist and he was first-generation immigrant to the US and computer science got him a pretty well-paying job, financial security, a family, and a house and so he was very adamant that I walked away in life with a skill I can fall back on."

"But I didn’t feel like I was as good or as smart as other people who dedicated their time 100% to STEM clearly. When I came to stanford, studying CS and majoring in CS became an option that I felt was more feasible, partly because the instructors in the classes I took encouraged me to keep doing it. I think I came in with a huge inferiority complex about my ability to perform in STEM, and I remember the first time or after the first quarter after getting an A in a CS class, having Keith write me an email and say “Hey, you did a pretty good job here. Maybe you should consider keeping studying this subject,” was definitely a strong factor in my decision to keep going with this."

"I just cared that I was in this field studying these problems, engaging in this life, and so I declared chemistry. And CS came quickly after that because it still is that coding is still something that I really like to do. It does put my at peace in a way that sometimes it’s hard to find in a busy life. "

Overall High School Demographics

"I feel like I came from a more racially diverse neighborhood in the sense that there were white kids but also Asian kids and Latino kids. It was a very racially diverse place but in the context of high school, it didn’t feel diverse because the classes I was taking only allowed me to come into contact with the same 20-30 people who were all relatively high income, white, or East Asian."

Diversity in the Classroom

"I’m pretty sure diversity is the first thing I look for when I walk into a classroom. It is really important to me because I feel like it changes the classroom atmosphere and it affects the degree to which I feel like I have to make an extra effort to be present or assert myself in a space because it does require extra effort if you’re in a very homogeneous room."

In CS classes, I think CS does a better job of being diverse because it is such a large major and so many people are drawn to it and so I frequently feel that I’m able to be in an environment in which I’m not the only person of color, not the only queer person, not the only non-binary person in the room. But at the same time, it’s a major that is so large that people are more inclined to think about class or something academic than how they’re engaging with the people around them and so sometimes the importance of having that diversity in the room gets lost and subsumed in the daily grind and rush to finish that coding assignment you have not finished yet, whereas in chemistry, it’s so small that people actually stop and talk to each other. It’s more clear that people are prioritizing diversity in the room.

Tech/STEM-related Field Aspirations?

"I want to work in a STEM-related field clearly, this is my major, but I’m not an entrepreneurial type. I’m not the kind of person who’s like let’s drill down and get these internships so I can go to Google when we graduate. I kind of just want to be in academia for as long as I can. It’s also because I have the financial privilege to be saying this, but at the moment, making a lot of money in the future or right now is not as important to me as finding someplace where I feel at home, at peace with myself, and challenged and interested enough to keep engaging in the problems I want to be engaging in. and so prioritizing finding those spaces is a lot more important to me than being a part of any Silicon Valley culture. Honestly, it’s not appealing to me at all. "

Improving Diversity in Tech/STEM

"I guess the two things are paying attention to the little things and paying attention to the people. I think that in terms of people, role models make a huge difference. This is the first quarter in a chemistry class where I’ve had a non-binary TA, and that has been an amazing experience for me. It just affected me in ways that I didn’t think I could be affected, just seeing a successful queer person in that position made me realize that something like that is possible for myself."

"It’s just doing your best to make sure that the room is representative is the core thing that it boils down to. And in terms of the little things, I think that it’s important to remember how you are affecting people even when you don’t realize you’re affecting people, even paying attention to how you are reacting engaging with someone at office hours or paying attention to how you are creating the forms you send out to people, how you’re addressing people, paying special attention to make sure that you’re using the preferred name or using the preferred pronouns. It’s all the little things that you don’t think add up to anything, in the grand scheme that actually add up to a lot."

Department Support

" But it’s just nice to feel that people know who you are and are watching after you, and obviously in the classroom, having TA’s who you feel like understand you, how you’re feeling in the room, what you’re dealing with on a day-to-day basis is also really important."

"In CS, it’s harder to express how I felt supported, probably because I haven’t looked for that as much. I feel like I’ve derived most of my support from, again, being in the Chemistry major, and so I felt less need to psychologically engage with the population in CS...I mean, CS has a lot of mentorship programs and there are mentorship programs for a lot of different forms of identity, and so if you want to be in contact with someone who identifies the same way you do and is also academically interested in the same things you’re interested in, it’s easy to connect with them and find them. Obviously professors and lecturers who reach out to you and take the time to get to know you, know your name, and know why you’re here, that also means a lot."
