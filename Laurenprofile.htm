LAUREN ZHU

Lauren Zhu (she/her) is a junior from San Diego majoring in Computer Science in the artificial intelligence track and is the co-president of Women in CS.

Interest in CS

“Growing up, I didn’t necessarily want to do CS and didn’t know anything about it. My parents are both CS people and seeing them sit in front of a computer all day wasn’t something I was interested in. However, I came into Stanford with an open mind and after I took 106A, I found it engaging and exciting. CS is an interesting way to apply logic and problems solving, and it’s cool to see them real time.”

“I am currently in the AI track. I want to work on something that’s really impactful, new and innovative.”

Industry Experiences

“For my freshman year summer, I interned at Qualcomm, working in the computer vision team. There were a lot of women on the team, and I really enjoyed that, as it was a very welcoming space, and I didn’t feel intimidated at all. For my sophomore year summer, I did research at the University of Edinburgh. There was definitely fewer women; my professor as well as most of team was men. It was more intimidating, but I had to realize that I know a lot and I have to be unafraid to step up and let them know I’m totally capable. I don’t feel hindered by being a minority in CS but am actually empowered by it.”

Diversity in Tech

“I can’t say I’ve had personal experience with race or gender discrimination but as president of [Women in CS] (WiCS), I definitely hear a lot about it. This year, I think that WiCS will focus our attention on creating awareness about [gender discrimination] and attempt to mitigate the effects. We want to introduce more panels with women who are higher up in the field and have had [these sorts of] experiences. For example, one person suggested a negotiation workshop to help [women stand up] for themselves and to emphasize their own worth.”

Increasing Diversity in Tech

“For Stanford, a lot of it is rooted within the CS department and organizations like WiCS which are trying to focus attention on supporting the broader community. The CS department is also trying to focus on promoting diversity within department and faculty.” “For industry, it is important to know that this is an issue that’s systemic within companies that need to be addressed. Many companies do have diversity groups which can be powerful and really important. Ultimately, it’s up to management of company to focus attention of these issues to be solved.”
