Readings, Research and Resources

  • General

  • Coraline Ada Ehmke "The Post-Meritocracy Manifesto"

    Discusses approaches to creating a more inclusive and socially responsible environment in STEM fields using a post-meritocracy approach.

  • Jonathan Akaba, Nina Prabhu, Shikha Srinivas, and Isabelle Zhou, "Bringing Social and Ethical Topics to STEM and Computer Science Classrooms "

    A post of social and ethical topics within CS and more broadly, STEM disciplines, to empower both students and teachers to foster a more inclusive STEM ecosystem through readings, website resources, and hands-on activities.

  • Noam Cohen, "We're All Nerds Now"

    Dives into the presence of geek culture within the tech industry, and how this culture can be dangerous when working to create an inclusive work community.

  • Women in STEM

  • Allison Master and Andrew N. Meltzoff, "Building bridges between psychological science and education: Cultural stereotypes, STEM, and equity"

    Research paper which contains information on barriers to women’s and girl’s interest in STEM, and possible avenues for solving the problem.

  • A.T. Wynn and S.J. Correll, "Puncturing the pipeline: Do technology companies alienate women in recruiting sessions?"

    A research paper that highlights and examines the factors that alienate women in recruiting sessions. Through gender-imbalanced presenter roles, geek culture references, overt use of gender stereotypes, and other gendered speech and actions, representatives may puncture the pipeline, lessening the interest of women at the point of recruitment into technology careers.

  • Emily Chang, Brotopia (supplementary)

    Contains information on the factors that shaped the current tech environment in Silicon Valley.

  • Godwin, Potvin, Hazari, Lock “Identity, Critical Agency, and Engineering: An Affective Model for Predicting Engineering as a Career Choice”

    Contains information on the push and pull factors when it comes to pursuing an engineering degree in college, and how this intersects with identity.

  • Ian Gent, "The Petrie Multiplier: Why an Attack on Sexism in Tech is NOT an Attack on Men"

    Outlines how sexist remarks disproportionately affects women in environments in which they are underrepresented.

  • Joy Rankin, Queens of Code

    Journal that discusses how the media portrays the gender inequality in Silicon Valley and how popular culture needs to be more aware of the way they are presenting gender.

  • Sepehr Vakil, "Ethics, Identity, and Political Vision Toward a Justice-Centered Approach to Equity in Computer Science Education" ??

    This paper contains useful problem solving approaches for issues of inclusion within high school CS curriculum. The information here is also relevant to the overall pipeline problem.

  • Susan Sibley “Why Do So Many Women Who Study Engineering Leave the Field?”

    Contains highly relevant information on some of the sociocultural forces that push women away from STEM in college and beyond. The research behind the paper also consisted of interviews with women in which they were asked about their experiences as engineering majors on campus.

  • Tracy Chou, "“I had so many advantages, and I barely made it”: Pinterest engineer on Silicon Valley sexism"

    This article gives a real world example of the struggle to fit in and feel accepted in a tech work space, coming from a minoritized group. Chou analyzes how sexism did not begin in the workforce, but was present in education.

  • USA Today "A feminist glossary"

    An article that defines words related to gender and sexism in the world today. It also defines various types and waves of feminism.

  • POC in STEM

  • Alona King, "No, I Am Not Lost A Black woman’s experience in the Stanford Computer Science Major", Ben Williams, "Debugging Code(Switching): My Experiences as a Black Computer Science Student at Stanford University"

    These articles share the experiences of minoritized groups in STEM on Stanford’s campus. The article gives examples of times where exclusion does occur on our own campus, but also expands to discuss the issue in a broader scale.

  • Betsy DiSalvo et al. "African American Males Constructing Computing Identity"

    Has useful information on the barriers to entry that prevent African American males from entering STEM fields, and possible remedies for breaking these barriers down.

  • Peggy McIntosh, "White Privilege: Unpacking the Invisible Knapsack"

    A guidebook that defines white privilege, helping others understand what it means and the impact it has upon society.

  • Kat Li, "The Invisible Minority of the Tech World"

    This article describes the experience of a Native person in tech and the lack of recognition of Native people's existence in the industry or in diversity data. This includes feeling invisible, feeling impostor syndrome, and having their Native identity being questioned.
