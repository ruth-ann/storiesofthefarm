SYDNEY JOHNSON

Sydney Johnson (she/her) is a junior majoring in chemical engineering.

Background for interest in Chemical Engineering

"I got into chemical engineering maybe in my senior year of high school. I was in a STEM magnet program, that allowed us to do basically research, and work at hospitals and other types of places, wherever you want to study. We would do research projects and in my senior year, I was working at a manufacturing facility where they manufacture different types of chemicals. I was working directly under a chemical engineer and a lot of people on his team were also chemical engineers, and they told me about the flexibility of the major. Getting that industry experience and a little bit of research helped me get into it, and then I came here and decided to continue it. My dad also graduated as a chemical engineer for his undergraduate degree.”

Early Mentors and Influences

“One of my mentors, who was also my physics teacher, encouraged me to apply for one of the science governor’s honors program in Georgia and she helped to prepare me. She was also in science club. I became involved in that, worked with her and eventually became the vice president, and she was a mentor for me in the sense of finding opportunities for me in STEM. In addition, when I was a little girl, I looked up to Mae Jemison who was an astronaut, and I found out later that she was a chemical engineer who graduated from Stanford. She also graduated with African and African American Studies, and she’s super awesome. I think it was a combination of all those things that made me want to look into STEM and engineering. I also just really liked math. I like solving problems. I kind of liked chemistry, but I loved math more and I think combining all of those things made me want to be an engineer."

STEM Classes Back Home

"I went to a public high school, and it was technically out of my district so I had to apply to get into the high school’s program. It wasn’t too far from me which wasn’t bad, but I had to catch the bus at different spots with kids who were also in other programs for different schools. It was a STEM magnet program within the public school. So there was a public school curriculum, and there was this magnet curriculum that was more STEM focused, and it was considered to be more advanced and more fast-paced. We took biology, chemistry, math and then our sophomore year is where we took more science and math. In our junior year, we took the AP Calculus classes, AP Chemistry, AP Physics, all of that. Senior year is when I started to take multivariable calculus, I took advanced mathematical topics which talked about proofs and graphs, which was really cool to me. I also took a beginning programming class where I learned about html and a little bit about Java, but not a whole lot. The rest of my courses were pretty well taught, especially my math courses. I really liked math and it really helped that I had teachers who challenged me and other students."

"In terms of demographics, there were maybe five or six black women in the program in my year, and there was maybe about fifty of us per year, and it was mostly white and Asian and a small percentage were black and Latinx students. Gender-wise, it felt pretty 50-50 if not 60-40 male to female. I remember in my advanced mathematical topics class, that’s where I really noticed it. There were around 20 students and maybe three or four of us were women."

"For me, I think as a black female student in the class, I was just kind of focused on my work, and I tried not to think about a lot of things externally, but I did feel sometimes, kind of awkward asking a question in class for instance, because I didn’t want to look dumb. I think in the multivariable and advanced mathematical topics classes, I think I was the only black female in my class, or maybe one of two or three black students. Sometimes I was kind of conscious that it was a majority white and Asian room, but I don’t think I felt severely conscious of my identity, I was just doing my work."

Transition to Stanford

"I’d say for my freshman math and chemistry classes, [my high school] prepared me really well. For instance in CME 100, a lot of it was covered in my high school so I felt comfortable with the transition. I don’t think I was used to the level of load for the psets, because the way my school operated for math classes was that they never collected homework. You were just expected to do it, and that’s how you performed on the exam. It was always about accountability on your end. Here, they force you to do your homework and it’s a graded category, and I think combined with other classes it felt a lot more rigorous. I was already comfortable with the material so it helped me."

"For me, I realized that if I didn’t have the best background knowledge in this, it would have been harder to navigate some of the critical thinking and problem solving questions. Sometimes they don’t really test if you know the material, they test if you know it and you can navigate a certain scenario which is kind of difficult sometimes. I would say freshman year, the transition was about getting used to the pace of things and that it was a different type of curriculum."

"Spring quarter, I took CME 104 and that was very different, very hard to understand. I had a great Additional Calculus for Engineers (ACE) teaching assistant (TA) so that helped. I also had organic chemistry. The first half or so of freshman year-- I remember emailing my high school teacher saying, 'You guys prepared me.'"

Experience Doing a Chemical Engineering Major as a Black Woman

"The content is very appealing to me, but some of the classes I’m required to take don’t interest me, because I’m interested in a very specific vein of chemical engineering. I’m interested in energy, and here they really push biology a lot-- I don’t like biology at all so sometimes it’s kind of tough in that sense. I do wish I hadn’t made up my mind so soon before coming to Stanford, because I think it would have been good to explore other majors, or other intro classes. That being said, I think I probably would still end up doing chemical engineering. I think my experience has just been that I’m kind of frustrated that a lot of the work is very theoretical and it’s not the most practical. A lot of the classes aren’t project oriented. It’s the one of the heaviest unit majors in the School of Engineering to my knowledge. We have a lot of prerequisites, a lot of chemistry that we probably don’t need. I’m happy that the department is making changes going forward, but there are still instances where even the way they sequence courses, it puts people at a disadvantage if you’re not prepared for the level of rigor. For instance, if you came in doing Chemistry 31A like I did as an incoming freshman, you would later on have to take a prerequisite course with a course that requires that prerequisite which is whack. I don’t know all the dynamics, but I think there’s a lot of progress to be made. I try to talk to people in the school of engineering and the student services people about it."

"As a black woman here at Stanford, I am hyper-aware of who I am, where I am, and my identity, because people let me know (laughs). Not that I didn’t feel it before in high school, but in high school I would hear my peers make jokes about black people, and I’d say, “That’s not okay, don’t say that.” People would say, “She only got into Stanford because she’s a black woman” and “Oh, it must be nice to be a black woman these days in the college process”, and I’d think, that’s not how it works. But back to Stanford, I’m hyper aware. There are only three black chemical engineers. There are twenty of us. About half of the people in chemical engineering are women, which is good but for me, I’m still learning to navigate certain dynamics. Even just forming study groups. I know no one will reach out to me. I have to be the one who says, “Hey, let’s work together,” or, “Let’s build a group."

"Some people have said, that we should meet up [to study], but I have to make sure that I follow through because they probably won’t do it. Or the fact that I have had TAs that have been very condescending to me based on my personal interpretation, or they’ve made fun of me for asking questions, but they won’t do that for other people. Or I have had some peers try to talk over me during office hours. Even little things, like when professors say, “Oh my goodness, you’ve changed your hair, I thought I had a whole new student.” And I’m like, “No, I just took my braids down.” It’s just like little things like that-- microaggressions."

"That being said, I have had professors who I felt were really supportive. I have met great people here like Professor Will Tarpeh (Assistant Professor of Chemical Engineering) who graduated from Stanford. He was in Talisman, he was also chemical engineering, he’s also black, he was in SBSE (Stanford Black Society for Engineers). He’s been a great resource and someone to talk to. It’s just kind of hard. I’m even now learning how to ask questions without feeling dumb in class. It doesn’t matter how I look to my peers at this point because I’m getting the same work done, and it’s for my understanding. Even freshman year, my motto was, “Let them tell me no.” I’m going to ask before I tell myself no for some things, and that’s something that I’m also working on. It’s an interesting dynamic."

"I try to focus on my work as much as possible because if I get too caught up stereotype threat or over-analyzing things, it will just be a lot to process and think about, when I’m just here to be a student. Others don’t have to worry about that. I need to just chill and focus on my work. I used to feel hyper-aware, but now I’m trying not to care as much. I am using as many resources as I can-- going to office hours more and being forward with what I want to get done. I’m not going to feel bad about asking a question. I used to ask the TA a question and say, “sorry, but-” and then ask, but there is really no need to apologize- this is their job. There are studies about women apologizing in certain contexts, and I’m learning not to apologize as much anymore, because there are more dynamics at play."

"I remember even in my CME 104 class during freshman year, I think it was majority male. It was maybe like 65-35. For one of my classes, I did a study where I looked at gender. I looked at the women who raised their hands and the men who raised their hands, and also speaking volume. I remember sitting in class, and I was taking notes for the class and also taking notes for the study for this other class. I noticed that I would hear the other girls whispering the same answer the guy would say when he raised his hand. Even for me, I would do the same thing where I’d have the answer, but it’s so hard to work up the courage sometimes to raise your hand because you just don’t want to be wrong. Other guys aren’t afraid to be wrong. Sometimes professors create this environment where they call the same people, and they allow them to do whatever. It’s hard to figure out whose fault it is, but it makes me think that if I were a professor, I’d be more conscious that not just this one person has the answer, I would actively choose other people."

"I also notice my peers try to double or triple check my answers or over verify with someone else to validate my answers when working on psets. I try not to focus on it too much, but I do notice it."

Support Networks

"SBSE has provided a great network of being able to reach out to people. I remember when I met Ariel Reid who was a coterm when I was in my first year of chemical engineering. It was great talking to her. Kevin Tynes, I still talk to him to this day. He (17:36) was president when I was a freshman, he was also chemical engineer. When I was in one other student group, there was another person, the only black chemical female chemical engineer in her year-- Kierra Franklin. She was also in SBSE, so I had a chance to talk to her. Will Tarpeh has been a great resource, the Stanford Summer Engineering Academy (SSEA) program has been especially helpful for me, because that helped further my transition to Stanford even better. I got to meet professors that way, and Dr. Noe Lozano (Associate Dean for Diversity at the School of Engineering) was also a helpful resource because he organizes the whole program. So for instance, I took a chemical engineering faculty to lunch, Professor Thomas Jaramillo, and then I had him later on as a professor. It was really helpful in that way. For him, also being a person of color in STEM too, that’s been a way of connection. I also work in the Office of Student Affairs, and so I have been able to talk with Dr. Lozano and now that he is retired, being able to talk to Lourdes Andrade (Director of Diversity and Inclusion in the School of Engineering). The whole office is full of women, which is really great. That’s also a very encouraging and very welcoming environment. Dean Thomas Kenny (Senior Associate Dean For Student Affairs In The School Of Engineering) also comes into the office sometimes too, and I’ll just talk to him about majors, and classes and he may ask me about my department. I feel pretty comfortable doing that given the connections I’ve had."

Thoughts on Creating a More Inclusive Environment

"I think just listening to us is the first step. We had a symposium where is was nice to talk to some of the people who develop our courses. We were able to talk to them about the issues we’ve had with it. I don’t know what they’re going to do with that information, but at least they gave us an opportunity. There’s John Whitney (Student Services Specialist for Undergrads, Masters, Coterms, Chemical Engineering), who I think has been really helpful in publicizing a lot of opportunities and so was the person before him. I learned via him, who learned about it via Lisa Hwang who is also in the program as well about the MIT Access program. This program sends students to MIT to learn about their PhD programs. That was a really good opportunity for me to meet other people from all over the country and also learn about PhD programs and graduate schools. I think they’re on their way, but it’s just a process."

"So just listening to the students and having a pulse on industry. I really want them to do a track system like MIT, so we don’t have to take all the classes, we can just take the classes that are relevant to us."

Memorable Experience in the STEM Classroom

" I remember when I was in Professor Jaramillo’s CHEMENG 100: Chemical Process Modeling, Dynamics, and Control class-- basically you learn how to use mathematical equations to model like temperature flow or concentration of a chemical. That was really interesting to me. I really enjoyed the class, also because I like math. I really like the idea of being able to take an equation, and adjust it and apply chemical properties to it-- like rates and reactions-- and be able to say that this is what a system does. I remember once 22.18 after class talking to Professor Harmio, and I said, “I think I want to be a chemical engineer for sure.” It was a really good moment for me."

Avenues for Major Exploration

"I’m still learning how to do that, but I would say my internship with the Sustainability Center after my freshman year allowed me to see a different side of renewable energy. I was kind of interested in hydrogen fuel cells before coming to Stanford based on an article I read and the SSEA program. The internship allowed me to see more about renewable energy. It’s funny because I learned about the internship via an SBSE meeting, and so I thought I should apply to it. So I applied, along with my other friend in SBSE, and so it was us working together with a group of other people. We had different majors, but they were all connected to renewable energy. So they included chemical engineering, materials science, human biology, civil and environmental engineering. We studied the factors that fall into the implementation of floating solar cells. So if you put it on a dam or a lake and you have floating devices with solar cells on them, and then also, how would you come up with a plan to implement a biodiesel processor at the educational forum, and being able to work in the lab and model what it looks like, and make our own biodiesel. We had a very small amount, but it was a really cool process, because although there were some STEM-y aspects to it, there are so many more policies and other background information that goes into it. Especially looking at how to store energy properly, and sending energy back to the grid, and how that works with public utilities, and how that works with refiners for biodiesels, how it’s processed, whether it’s economical or environmentally friendly. Knowing that there are so many dimensions to the work you do as an engineer, it made me really interested in being maybe someone in the future who is creating cool technology, but is also a liaison between the communities and the government. There are a lot of gaps between the people who make policies and those who build tech."

Attraction to Energy

"For me, energy is interesting because it fuels everything. You need petroleum engineering to get the fuel for most cars these days. For energy, there are so many dimensions and aspects to it. There are renewables, there is traditional, electrical. I was really interested in hydrogen fuel cells in particular, even water splitting processes where you can take water molecules and you can capture the hydrogen and use that to fuel cars. I’m more interested in the idea of sustainable, clean energy, because it seems good for the earth, and if it’s used in the right way, it can help a lot of people."

Career Aspirations After Graduating

"It’s kind of tricky figuring out what to do for the future. Right now I’m thinking I’ll probably do grad school, I’m not sure where exactly or if it’s going to end at a Master’s or if it’s going to end at a PhD.I think that I want to do grad school, maybe work in industry, then eventually get to the point where I’m working as a liaison. I feel like a lot of the clean energy is being focused in higher income areas-- so making sure that there is accessible clean energy for other people, and also in even developing countries. As economies are progressing, there is still a lot of reliance on coal, gas and oil, which isn’t bad, but as everyone is trying to find more renewable energy solutions, I want to make sure that it’s more evenly distributed. I’m not sure how I’m going to get there yet, but I’m learning as I go. I definitely want to work in energy, but this is what I’m thinking now, but I know that in five years I may be thinking doing completely different."

Thoughts on Working in Silicon Valley, and Perceptions

"I’m originally from Texas and I’ve lived in Michigan, and my family and I currently live in the Atlanta area in Georgia. I like being close to family. Although the Bay Area has it’s pros, I will probably be somewhere in Chicago, Atlanta or Houston"

"I imagine it to be very white. I imagine people sitting at a computer all day. I know that there is diversity there, but it often times just doesn’t feel inclusive and if there are diversity or affinity groups, it sometimes feels kind of false and inauthentic. That being said, every workplace has its issues, and microaggressions-- sexism, racism-- isn’t new to any workplace. Being here specifically around emerging technology, there are a lot of voices that are being left out, and that affects how for instance, in Airbnb, black people are staying at a house that they rented and the cops were called on them. So those little things. I guess I’m trying to be a bit more socially conscious about the intents of companies as well because some things seem like they’re doing well, but they may be supporting something that’s counter-intuitive."

"Additionally I used to mentor at a middle school. The program is called WYSE (Women and Youth Supporting Each Other), and it was focused on facilitating discussions about all sorts of things related to the students so whether it’s career aspirations, puberty, relationships, women representation or racism and stuff like that. It’s interesting because the middle school we were at is now shut down, and other schools are shutting down because of gentrification and companies coming in. They’ll create their own new school, which feels counter-intuitive. Or they will go into these schools and create makerspaces, but then they are the same companies that are also pushing community members out based on rent prices and stuff like that. There is a lot going on, and it’s not new to the Bay Area or to Silicon Valley. It’s just more prevalent to me living in this area, and sometimes I forget about it because we are in a bubble. The same thing is happening all over the country."

Closing Thoughts

"I’m really excited about a program I’m in this year called seeds for change. It’s basically a research center, but it’s also a leadership program and mentorship activity. I get to mentor these female high school students, and we just discuss different strategies, and being a woman in STEM. With intersectionality, we talk about being women of color too. So different strategies in how to navigate certain things. I’m really excited about the program because I learn and the students learn. It’s a cool environment. "
